Uniform Price Dual Cap Batch Auction

Auction repair happens after fork migration. If a disputed market cannot resolve in the parent universe, callers can create child pools for selected valid fork branches.

Each child pool uses REP from its child universe. A truth auction sells that REP for ETH so the child pool can repair collateral. The auction repairs solvency. It does not decide which branch is true.

At that point, the parent pool has stopped in migration, callers can ask the forker to create child pools for selected valid outcomes, and the auction is the optional ETH-repair phase for a child pool that still needs collateral.

SecurityPoolForker uses this auction when a forked child pool needs ETH collateral repair.

The implementation path centers on UniformPriceDualCapBatchAuction and the forker settlement wrapper. The broader fork-recovery story lives in the truth auction section in the Placeholder whitepaper.

Why This Auction Exists

After a universe fork, a child pool can inherit less ETH collateral than it needs to continue normal operation. The forker can sell some child-universe REP for ETH to repair that collateral gap. This keeps trader share collateral separate from the question of which branch is true.

The design is dual-capped: it tries to raise enough ETH without selling more REP than the child pool is allowed to sell. If demand is weak, the auction sells only what qualifying bids demand and leaves unsold REP in the pool instead of forcing a low-price full sale.

Operational Lifecycle

The auction is owned by the coordinating SecurityPoolForker, not by the bidder. That ownership is intentional because purchased REP is credited into child-pool vault accounting instead of being transferred directly from the auction to the bidder.

A truth auction can start only for a child pool in ForkMigration after the parent migration window has ended. If enough REP migrated to the child pool already, the forker can finalize the repair path without starting an auction.

Gas and Spam Bounds

The auction intentionally does not cap total bids or active price levels. A cap would let an attacker spend ETH to fill the available bid slots or tick slots early, preventing later valid bidders from participating.

Finalization remains bounded without those caps because it does not iterate over raw bids. Same-tick bids append to per-tick arrays for later paged withdrawal, while clearing uses aggregate ETH totals stored in an AVL tree keyed by tick. The valid tick range is finite: [-524288, 524288], or 1,048,577 possible price levels. An AVL tree containing every possible tick has maximum height 28, so finalize() descends bounded tree paths and pruned subtrees.

The gas tests cover both funded and underfunded finalization over a synthetic max-depth tree for the full tick domain. They assert each path remains below 20,000,000 gas and print measured gas values in the test output.

Refund and Settlement Paths

Clearly losing bids can be refunded before finalization once current demand is enough to find a clearing tick. Only ticks below that found clearing tick can be withdrawn through the pre-finalization refund path; binding or potentially winning bids stay in the auction.

In the underfunded branch, finalization computes underfundedThreshold from aggregate accepted bid ETH, and only ticks whose tickToPrice value is at or above that threshold win. The contract's stored ethRaised value is the threshold input; underfundedWinningEth is the ETH actually retained or sent after below-threshold bids are refunded.

underfundedThreshold = floor ( ethRaised ⋅ PRICE_PRECISION maxRepBeingSold )

Underfunded ThresholdPRICE_PRECISION = 1e18. A tick wins when tickToPrice(tick) >= underfundedThreshold, so equality wins. Solidity division floors the threshold, REP demand, and per-bid allocation; remainder values carry forward so paged withdrawals reconcile to the aggregate tick result.

Finalized case Bid treatment Why it works this way
Losing bids Receive ETH refunds. They did not contribute to the accepted clearing demand.
Normal winning bids above clearing tick Convert all accepted ETH into purchased REP at the uniform clearing price. Higher bids should receive the same execution price as the marginal winner.
Normal marginal clearing tick May be partially filled and partially refunded; same-tick bids fill FIFO by submission order. FIFO gives deterministic marginal allocation without iterating over all same-tick bids during finalization.
Underfunded winning ticks Ticks at or above underfundedThreshold buy only their limit-priced REP demand; same-tick bids share that demand pro rata by ETH. Weak demand should leave REP unsold instead of forcing a lower uniform full-sale price.

Zero-effective-price ticks are rejected when bids are submitted, so underfunded settlement cannot assign REP to bids whose rounded ETH/REP price is zero.

Both finalized allocation paths carry division dust across paged withdrawals. Normal clearing carries clearingRemainder through the uniform-price ETH-to-REP division, and underfunded clearing carries a per-tick remainder through the same-tick ETH-to-REP allocation. Later withdrawals at the same tick may receive remainder carried from earlier integer division so bid-level withdrawals reconcile to the aggregate finalized REP demanded at that tick.

Worked Clearing Example

Suppose the auction tries to raise 12 ETH while selling at most 4 REP. Alice bids 3 ETH near 5 ETH/REP, Bob bids 4 ETH near 4 ETH/REP, and Carol bids 6 ETH near 3 ETH/REP.

repWon = filledEth clearingPrice

Auction FillIn the funded path, every filled bid settles at the same clearing price; only the amount of ETH filled can differ at the marginal tick.

Bidder Bid Settlement at 3 ETH/REP
Alice 3 ETH near 5 ETH/REP Fully filled; receives 1 REP.
Bob 4 ETH near 4 ETH/REP Fully filled; receives 4/3 REP.
Carol 6 ETH near 3 ETH/REP Partially filled; receives 5/3 REP and a 1 ETH refund.

The auction raises 12 ETH and sells 4 REP. Everyone who wins receives the same effective execution price, while only the marginal bid is clipped.
