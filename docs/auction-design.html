Truth Auction

A truth auction is held only after a fork. That fork can happen because the market's own escalation game reaches non-decision, or because some other market forks Zoltar first and this pool is later migrated as part of the same child-universe split. After either path, Placeholder migrates it into child pools for the valid fork branches that users want to keep using. Each migrated child pool can then run its own separate truth auction inside that child universe if that branch still needs collateral repair.

Fork migration preserves disagreement by splitting one parent market into multiple child branches, but ETH does not get copied into every child pool in full. Each branch inherits only the ETH implied by the parent collateral and migrated REP state that actually move into that branch, with parent-universe REP backing converted into the REP of the child universe. A child pool can therefore inherit the correct migrated share balances, vault state, and REP-backed obligations, yet still hold too little ETH to reopen cleanly on day one. When that happens, the protocol attempts to sell some child-universe REP for ETH so the missing collateral can be restored and open-interest holders can be made whole. The truth auction repairs that collateral gap; it does not decide which branch is true.

Placeholder implements the truth auction with the UniformPriceDualCapBatchAuction contract. After migration, that auction opens, clears bids, and feeds winning results back into vault state instead of paying out direct token transfers. A winning claim becomes child-pool ownership plus the matching share of auctioned security-bond allowance, so bidders are buying into the repaired child vault state rather than receiving a bare REP transfer.

Why This Auction Exists

After a universe fork, a child pool can inherit less ETH collateral than it needs to continue normal operation even when its migrated shares, vault state, and child-universe REP backing are correct. The shortfall comes from proportional migration. ETH moves into each child branch in the same ratio as migrated REP, not as a full copy of the parent pool's collateral. A child can therefore inherit the migrated shares, pool ownership, and security-bond allowance it needs to account for, yet still have too little ETH to reopen because only the proportional slice of parent collateral followed that branch.

To close that gap, REP is offered for sale inside the child universe. Each child computes its own ETH shortfall from the parent collateral that migrated with its REP. If a child remains short after migration, that child runs its own separate auction inside its own universe.

The design is dual-capped because the auction has two limits at once: it is trying to raise a fixed amount of ETH, and it is not allowed to sell more than a fixed amount of child-universe REP. Strong demand can hit the ETH target first, or it can bind the REP sale cap first. In that REP-capped case, the next accepted price would sell at or beyond the REP limit, so final purchased REP can land at or just below the cap because ticks and Solidity division are discrete. Weak demand can leave the sale underfunded, in which case some REP remains unsold instead of being forced out at a worse price. Operational Lifecycle

The truth auction starts only after the migration window has ended. That window lasts SecurityPoolUtils.MIGRATION_TIME (8 weeks), measured from the parent universe fork time. If the migrated branch is still short of ETH after that window, the auction opens. If the child pool has no remaining ETH collateral gap after migration, the repair path can finalize without starting an auction.

Once the repair path is open, bidders supply ETH directly to the auction. Settlement then credits purchased REP into child-pool vault accounting instead of transferring tokens directly to the bidder, so the repair result feeds back into the pool rather than bypassing it. The same settlement also assigns a proportional slice of the auctioned security-bond allowance, which is the debt-bearing side of the migrated vault accounting that did not follow REP cleanly into the child branch.

How Clearing Works

Bidders submit ETH at discrete price ticks, where each tick encodes an ETH-per-REP limit price. The auction then walks demand from the highest price downward. A bid above the clearing tick wins in full, a bid below it loses in full, and bids at the clearing tick share the last fill. In the uniform-clearing branch that last fill is First in First Out within the tick, so earlier same-tick bids are consumed before later ones.

The tick math follows the contract's tickToPrice function. Tick 0 means 1 ETH/REP. Each step up multiplies price by roughly 1.0001, and each step down divides by the same factor, so the price at tick tickIndex is approximately 1.0001tickIndex ETH/REP. In other words, a higher tick means the bidder is willing to pay more ETH per REP, which is why higher ticks are evaluated first during clearing.

Two caps define the uniform-clearing stopping point. The first cap is ethRaiseCap, the amount of ETH the child pool is trying to restore. The second cap is maxRepBeingSold, the maximum child-universe REP inventory the pool is willing to sell. As soon as cumulative demand is enough to hit either cap at some price, that price becomes the clearing tick and all winning bids settle from it at one price. That is a valid uniform clearing even when the REP cap binds before the ETH repair target is fully met. The contract finds that result by descending aggregate subtree totals, not by looping over every bid one by one.

When demand never reaches a full clearing price, the auction switches into the underfunded branch. It computes one threshold price from total accepted ETH and available REP, then only bids at or above that threshold win. Those winning ticks buy only the REP they demanded at their own limit prices, which is why weak demand leaves some REP unsold instead of forcing a lower uniform full-sale price.

Gas and Spam Bounds

The main spam risk is a bidder trying to fill bid slots or tick levels cheaply enough to block later demand. The auction avoids that failure mode by not capping total bids or active price levels at all.

Finalization remains bounded without those caps because it does not iterate over raw bids. Same-tick bids append to per-tick arrays for later paged withdrawal, while clearing uses aggregate ETH totals stored in an AVL tree keyed by tick. The tree exists so the contract can keep all valid price levels open during bidding, yet still find the highest winning region by descending aggregate subtree totals instead of scanning every submitted bid. Finalization therefore pays for tree descent across the finite tick domain rather than for the total number of bids in the auction. The valid tick range is finite: [-524288, 524288], or 1,048,577 possible price levels. An AVL tree containing every possible tick has maximum height 28, so finalize() descends bounded tree paths and pruned subtrees instead of risking an out-of-gas loop over every submitted bid.

The gas tests cover both funded and underfunded finalization over a synthetic max-depth tree for the full tick domain. They assert each path remains below 20,000,000 gas and print measured gas values in the test output, so the documented worst case is a max-depth AVL-tree finalization path that still stays under that bound.

Settlement Modes

Currently losing bids can be refunded before finalization once current demand is enough to find a clearing tick. Only ticks below that found clearing tick can be withdrawn through the pre-finalization refund path; binding or potentially winning bids stay in the auction. In the underfunded branch, finalization computes underfundedThreshold from aggregate bid ETH still in the tree at finalization time, and only ticks whose tickToPrice value is at or above that threshold win. The contract's stored ethRaised value is that threshold input; underfundedWinningEth is the ETH actually retained or sent after below-threshold bids are refunded. This is not a self-correcting fixed point: low-price bids can push the threshold up only by locking ETH that may later be refunded, which the mechanism treats as an accepted capital-lock tradeoff.

underfundedThreshold = floor ( ethRaised ⋅ PRICE_PRECISION maxRepBeingSold )

That settlement is why a winning bid buys more than a token transfer. The forker withdraws finalized bid results, converts purchased REP into pool ownership with auctionPoolOwnershipPerRep, and adds the matching slice of auctionedSecurityBondAllowance to the bidder's vault. In other words, the bidder is buying a share of the repaired child-pool vault state, including the debt-bearing allowance that still has to be backed.

Zero-effective-price ticks are rejected when bids are submitted, so underfunded settlement cannot assign REP to bids whose rounded ETH/REP price is zero.

Both finalized allocation paths carry division dust across paged withdrawals. Normal clearing carries clearingRemainder through the uniform-price ETH-to-REP division, and underfunded clearing carries a per-tick remainder through the same-tick ETH-to-REP allocation. Later withdrawals at the same tick may receive remainder carried from earlier integer division so bid-level withdrawals reconcile to the aggregate finalized REP demanded at that tick.
